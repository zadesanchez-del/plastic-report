Part 01

Film Evidence
from the field.

Three Examples — Human Impact
  1. Approximately 8 million tons of plastic dumped into the ocean every year, mostly from rivers carrying trash from cities.
  2. Single-use plastic packaging — bottles, bags, wrappers — piling up on beaches like Smith Island in the Philippines.
  3. Lost fishing nets ("ghost nets") that keep trapping and killing fish, turtles, and whales long after they're abandoned.
Two Scientific Processes

Bioaccumulation

Toxins from plastic move up the food chain: plankton → small fish → big fish → humans. The poison concentrates at every step.

Ocean Gyre Circulation

Currents trap floating plastic in five massive rotating zones, including the infamous Great Pacific Garbage Patch.

Two Abiotic Factors Affected

Dissolved Oxygen

Drops as microbes consuming plastic-related debris use up the available oxygen in the water column.

Light Penetration

Falls when floating plastic blocks sunlight from reaching deeper water where producers depend on it.

Two Pieces of Evidence
Quantitative

Researchers found 6× more plastic than plankton by weight in some North Pacific Gyre water samples.

Observational

Dead seabirds — especially albatross chicks on Midway Atoll — had stomachs packed with bottle caps and plastic fragments.

How Plastic Enters Aquatic Systems

Mostly through rivers carrying trash from land, plus dumping from ships, lost fishing gear, and microfibers shed from washing synthetic clothes.

Organisms Most Affected

Filter feeders like whales and oysters; surface feeders like seabirds and turtles; and apex predators like tuna and sharks. They either swallow plastic by accident or accumulate concentrated toxins from prey lower in the food chain.

Every aquatic ecosystem shown — open ocean, coral reefs, rivers, even Arctic ice — has plastic in it. No ecosystem is plastic-free anymore.
Part 02

Scientific Analysis.

Effect on Food Webs & Energy Flow

Plastic blocks sunlight, so phytoplankton — the producers — make less energy through photosynthesis. That means less food at the base of the web, and every level above receives less energy in turn.

Producers

Less photosynthesis as light penetration drops.

Consumers

Eat plastic, feel full but starve; toxins build up in their bodies.

Decomposers

Can't break plastic down — it doesn't get recycled into nutrients like normal organic matter.

Carbon Cycle

Plastic is made from oil. Production releases buried CO₂ — about 850 million tons globally per year.

Nitrogen Cycle

The "plastisphere" — bacteria living on plastic surfaces — disrupts how nitrogen is normally cycled in seawater.

Temperature

Dark plastic absorbs sunlight and warms surrounding water, intensifying heating in shallow areas like reefs.

Trend Explanation

Northern Hemisphere oceans hold dramatically more microplastic than Southern Hemisphere ones — a direct consequence of population distribution. Most of the world's people live north of the equator, so most plastic waste enters there. Ocean currents then trap that plastic in spinning gyres.

The Mediterranean is exceptionally polluted because it's nearly enclosed: plastic enters but cannot escape. Even the Arctic now carries plastic, ferried in by currents and frozen into ice.

Part 04

Case Study: Microplastics.

Cause — Human Activity

Microplastics come from two sources. Primary microplastics are manufactured small on purpose — microbeads in face wash, synthetic clothing fibers shed in laundry. Secondary microplastics are larger plastics broken down by sun and waves. A single load of synthetic laundry can shed over 700,000 microfibers.

Short-Term Effects
  • Zooplankton and small fish eat them and starve, feeling full on plastic.
  • Sharp fragments injure internal organs.
  • Toxic chemicals like BPA leach into the water and into animal tissue.
Long-Term Effects
  • Toxins biomagnify as they move up the food chain.
  • Endocrine disruptors interfere with hormones, harming reproduction and development.
  • Microplastics now found in human blood, lungs, and placentas.
  • Plastic doesn't biodegrade — pollution lasts 400+ years.
Evidence from the Film
Sample Data

Trawl samples in the North Pacific Gyre had 6× more plastic than plankton by weight.

Market Survey

Researchers cut open fish from regular fish markets and found microplastics inside.

Chemistry

Plastic absorbs toxic chemicals from seawater at concentrations 1 million times higher than the surrounding water.

Part 05

Engineering a Solution.

Proposal

River Interception System
+ Biodegradable Plastic Mandate

How It Works
  1. A V-shaped floating barrier (boom) stretches across a river. The current pushes plastic toward the center.
  2. At the center, a solar-powered conveyor belt lifts plastic out of the water onto land.
  3. An on-shore sorting facility separates plastics by type and recycles them. Non-recyclable material is converted to fuel.
  4. A government policy requires companies to transition to biodegradable plastics like PHA or PLA within 5 years.
Benefits
  • Catches plastic in rivers before it reaches the ocean — rivers carry 80% of ocean plastic.
  • Solar-powered, so no fossil fuels burned to operate.
  • Fish swim freely under the boom — no harm to wildlife.
  • 10× cheaper per ton than open-ocean cleanup.
  • Combines technology AND policy to attack both ends.
Limitations
  • Only catches floating plastic — sunken and dissolved microplastics escape.
  • Requires maintenance, especially after storms.
  • Bioplastics still need composting facilities to fully break down.
  • Hard to enforce in countries with weak environmental law.
Cost vs. Environmental Impact
Cost

~$1 million per system + $120,000/year to operate

Capture

50–100 tons of plastic per system per year

Pays for itself within 7–10 years through recycled plastic sales and reduced downstream cleanup costs.

Part 06

A CER
argument.

Prompt

What is the most effective way to reduce plastic pollution in aquatic ecosystems?

— Claim

The most effective way to reduce plastic pollution is a combined upstream approach: intercept plastic in rivers before it reaches the ocean, and require biodegradable alternatives through policy.

Evidence
From the Film

About 8 million tons of plastic enter the ocean each year, mostly through rivers, and once it's there, it breaks into microplastics that are nearly impossible to remove.

From Research

Just 1,000 rivers carry 80% of all ocean plastic — cleanup at those rivers stops most of the problem at the source, at 1/10 the cost of ocean cleanup.

Scientific Concept

Toxins from plastic biomagnify ~10× at each level of the food chain. Every piece prevented from entering the ocean stops thousands of toxic exposures upstream in the food web.

Reasoning

Prevention is far more effective than cleanup. Once plastic is in the ocean, currents disperse it across millions of square miles and break it into pieces that last 400+ years. Stopping plastic at rivers — narrow chokepoints — is much easier than chasing it across whole oceans.

Biodegradable plastics like PHA actually break down into CO₂, water, and biomass, so they re-enter natural cycles instead of polluting forever. Technology alone won't fix this because we keep producing more plastic. Policy alone won't fix existing waste. Combined, they attack both source and impact — protecting the food web, water quality, and biogeochemical cycles all at once.

No ecosystem is plastic-free anymore. But every ecosystem can still be saved — if we stop the plastic before it ever reaches the water.
